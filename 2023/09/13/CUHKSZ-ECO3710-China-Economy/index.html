Kuznets Curve

Possible Mechanisms for KC

Early in the development process, investment opportunities for those who have money multiply, while an influx of cheap rural labor to the cities holds down wages.

As economy further develops, human capital accrual takes the place of physical capital accrual as the main source of growth; and inequality slows growth by lowering education levels because poorer, disadvantaged people lack finance for their education in imperfect credit-markets. Financial market development will start to relieve the credit market imperfection, leading to more qual oppotunity in education.

Piketty

He argues that Kuznets mistook the 1930-1950 decrease in inequality for the endpoint of its development. Since 1950, inequality has again reached pre-WW II levels. Similar trends are visible in European countries.

  • Global income inequality, as measured by the ratio T10/B50 between the average income of the top 10% and the average income of the bottom 50%, more than doubled between 1820 and 1910, from less than 20 to about 40, and stabilized around 40 between 1910 and 2020.

Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

  • 如果财富或收入完全均等分配，那么洛伦兹曲线将是一条45度对角线（对应累积人口和累积财富或收入相等）。不平等的分配将导致曲线向上凸起，越凸出表示不平等越严重。——即基尼系数越接近1

  • 基尼系数最大为“1”，最小为“0”。前者表示居民之间的年收入分配绝对不平均（即该年所有收入都集中在一个人手里，其余的国民没有收入），而后者则表示居民之间的该年收入分配绝对平均，即人与人之间收入绝对平等，基尼系数的实际数值只能介于这两种极端情况，即0～1之间。要注意基尼系数只计算某一时段，如一年的收入，不计算已有资产，因此它不能反映国民的总积累财富分配情况。

基尼系数计算：$\frac{对角线与曲线间的面积}{对角线下的总面积}$

Walk through history: China’s development strategy

  • Hearvy industry-led development strategy in the 1950s
    • Violate CN’s comparative advantage at the time that capital was scarce and labor was abundant
    • Form Hukou (Household registration system) and create rural-urban gap
  • By the late 1970s, China had experienced the Great Famine and Cultural Revolution and its economy was at the verge of collapse

Rural Reform

  • Abolished the ineffecient collective farming
  • Rural household responsibility system - allow farms to make their own production decisions.

Open Up

When opening up, regional compariative advantage is evaluated in global context

  • In the late 1970s, China set up 14 special economic zones in the coast. Allow some people (region) to get rich first
  • Cheap labor and land, abundant capital from overseas made the coastal area a growth engine in the 1980s

Decentralization

Impact: Less transfers, more incentives

Limit: When there is large regional variation, decentralization may not work well; there is an increasing scale of economy due to the fixed cost of administration; With the presence of increasing scale, decentralization may force capital flow from poor to rich region

  • Expenditures tie more closely to revenue
  • Local government officials’ promotion is based on their performance in economic growth and revenue growth
  • Horizontal inter-judiciary competition serves as a commitment device for local governments to create more favorable investment environment

Coastal Region: Race to the Top

  • Condition
    • regions are similar
    • there is a mobile factor of capital
  • Consequence:
    • less tax on capital but more on land
    • better investment environment
    • many innovations at the local level

Two spirals

  • Tax burdens are extremely high in poor regions with agriculture as the major means of production
    • Downward spiral: small tax base, more extraction from limited agricultural surplus and nonfarm activities, worsening investing environment, and lowering public goods provision
  • They are low in developed regions initially with a large nonfarm sector
    • Virtuous cycle: light tax burden for each enterprise, more public inputs, better investing environment

Go west strategy

  • Started in 1999 after the Asian financial crisis
  • Massive infrastructure investment in the west
  • Set up Chongqing as a province-level city, enjoying the same status as Shanghai and Beijing

Other Regional Strategies

  • Rising Central 中部崛起
  • Reinvirorating Northeast 振兴东北
  • Setting up a new economic zone in Tianjin

New Socialism Countryside

  • Abolished agricultural taxation
  • Provided direct grain subsidies to farmers
  • Waived tuitions of 9-year basic education in vast rural areas
  • Increase public investment in rural areas

Conclusions

  • Development strategies and economic policies have affected the course of regional development patterns
  • Change in regional inequality match the pahses of Chinese history remarkably well.

Topic 5-2 Tale of Transition

Paper: A tale of transition: an empirical analysis of economic inequality in urban china, 1986-2009 (By: Haiyan Ding and Hui He)

History of Economic Inequality in China

  • The rapid growth of China’s economy in the past 3 decades has resulted in a widening economic inequality gap
    • Variance of log household disposable income in China increased from 0.14 to 0.43 in 1986-2009 - the fastest increase of any country covered in RED (Review of Economic Dynamics)
  • Unlike other countries, they find that consumption inequality tracks closely with income inequality over time (most other countries, like the US, have consumption inequality increasing slower than income inequality)
  • Why do they track closely? Two possible reasons:
    • Existence of “hand to mouth” consumers, i.e., people who consume what they earn, consumption equals income
    • A change in the income shock structure due to economic transition

Permanent income shock: long term change in income due to injury, job change, etc.

Transitory income shock: temporary change in income due to temp, sickness, leave, bonuses, etc.

Why is there a change in shock structure? Because of major Chinese Economic Events:

1978: Open-door policy initiated by Deng, economic reform

1984: After household responsibilities system reform in rural China, economic reform shifts focus to cities

1989: Economic crisis triggered after political turmoil following corruption and inflation

1992: Reform pushes China towards market economy, as privatization begins

1997: Private sector eventually surpasses state sector in GDP share, leading to SOE privatization through “grasp the large, let go of the small” policy

Paper Research Questions

  • How do consumption inequality and income inequality change over time in China?
  • What are the causes for these changes over time?

Their answers:

  • Income inequality and consumption inequality have been rapidly increase in China
  • Particularly surprising: co-movement between income and consumption inequality
  • Explanation: Permanent income shocks are dominating transitory income shocks as a result of economic transition in China, leading to less risk-sharing across individuals

Measures of Inequality

  2. Mini coefficient

income inequality general trends: increase in all 4 metrics

What’s driving this inequality? The rich are getting richer much faster than the poor are getting rich

Gov. response to inequality? Difference between gross income and pre-tax income is public pension benefits and other social security benefits

What can we learn from other countries?

  • For most countries, income inequality rises much faster than consumption inequality, which reflects the improvements in risk sharing in more developed economies
  • Overtime, consumers can smooth out their consumption relatively easy, leading to consumption inequality < income inequality
  • China is an anomaly

Breaking down consumption inequality

Non-durable consumption and Necessary non-durable consumption （非耐用品消费：日常商品/服务）较低水平、趋势很稳定

Durable consumption （耐用品消费：家具、电器、汽车等）水平最高，趋势最不稳定，波动上升

Social status consumption （社会地位消费）水平比非耐用品高，趋势稳定

Income Dynamics

  • Over time, there is very limited risk sharing across individuals
  • Thus, consumption inequality closely tracks with income inequality
  • Possible theories:
    • “Hand to mouth” consumers
    • Underlying income shock structure
      • Rising permanent income shocks dominate transitory income shocks over time

Theory 1: “Hand-to-Mouth”

  • Theory implies:
    • Consumption would be roughly equal to income
    • Variance is roughly equal
    • Saving rate should be close to 0
  • Problems:
    • Average household saving rate is close to 20% over time
    • Saving rate has been increasing over time

Except in the lowest income quintile, we see that the household saving rate is significantly positive in all income quintiles

Theory 2: Underlying Income Shock Structure

Conclusion and Further Recommendations

  • Drastic increase in economic inequality from 1986 to 2009
  • Consumption inequality closely tracks with income inequality over time
    • Goes against standard consumption smoothing theory
    • Estimate inequalities over the life cycle
  • Possible explanation is fundamental change in idiosyncratic income shock structure
    • More difficult for individuals to insure against income shocks
  • Could be “growing pain” associated with economic transition
