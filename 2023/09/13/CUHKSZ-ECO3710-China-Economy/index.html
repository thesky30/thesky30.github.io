How do we understand corruption?

Grabbing hand view

  • Corruption is a distortion and is costly to economic development
  • The “grabbing hands” increase the cost of operating business, leading to less entry on the extensive margin and to smaller scale on the intensive margin
  • Eliminating governments’ grabbing hands will lead to more firm entry and more profitable for existing firms and in general will improve economic efficiency

Greasing wheels view

  • In an environment with excessive bureaucratic burden, paying bribes may help avoid bureaucratic delays and make government officials work harder

Firms spend money to build connections

  • the effect of local political turnover on perk spending is stronger in areas with more corruption
  • the effect is weaker for SOEs or firms with more politically connected CEOs or Chairmen

Summary

  • A significant portion of ETC is bribes to government officals, both for protection against government expropriation and in exchange for better government service
  • Accordingly, in situations with more severe government expropriation and a lower quality of government service, ETC tends to be higher and have a higher marginal return to firm performance
  • Managers also spend a portion of ETC on managerial excesses, as shown by the fact that ETC have a significantly negative effect on firm performance

Conclusion

  • We use large unique firm-level data sets from China to not only detect but also investigate the institutional causes of corruption among Chinese firms
  • We also examine how the private returns to firms from corruptive activities vary with institutional factors
  • We rely on the predictions from a simple but plausible model of managerial decision-making to identify components of ETC by exaamining how the total ETC responds to different environmental variables
  • We find strong evidence that firms’ ETC consists of a mix that includes expenditures on government officials both as “grease money” and “protection money”, expenditures to build relational capital with suppliers and clients, and managerial excesses
    • ETC overall has a significantly negative effect on firm performance (measured by both profitability and productivity), but its negative effect is much less pronounced for those firms located in cities with low quality government service, those subject to severe government expropriation and those who do not have strong relationship with their suppliers and clients
    • We also find that the negative impact of ETC is less pronouced in firms with better corporate governance
  • Final note: while we find that parts of ETC expenditures have positive impact on firm performance, it does not necessarily imply that, these components of ETC expenditure are socially “efficient grease”

Topic 4-1 SOE _Reform__Entrepreneurship

Paper: Reluctant Entrepreneurs: Evidence from China’s SOE Reform

Motivation

  • Entrepreneurship is one of the core engines of economic growth
  • Entrepreneurs with higher skills tend to exhibit greater growth throughout their life cycles
  • The conventional wisdom that individuals with the greatest ability naturally gravitate towards entrepreneurship, irrespective of alternative job prospects, has been challenged
  • Reluctant entrepreneurs - High-skilled individuals may be reluctant to choose entrepreneurship when alternative sector offer attractive features
  • We study how the availability of SOEs as a career option impacts the career decisions of high-skilled individuals, particularly their choices between waged employment and entrepreneurship

Why China?

  • The share of SOEs in the Chinese economy has experienced dramatic changes since the 1990s. The number of state-owned and state-controlled enterprises fell from 118,000 in 1995 to 24,961 in 2004
  • Despite the fact that SOEs have lower productivity than POEs, SOEs have better access to economic resources, and SOE employees enjoy superior job-related benefits
  • China is unique in the sense that the State Administration for Industry and Commerce maintains the firm registration database which covers the universe of all registered firms established in China since 1949

This Paper

  • Use a simple three-sector occupational choice model to illustrate how individuals with different entrepreneurial ability select into three sectors and generates testable hypothesizes
  • Leverage a historical shock of the momentous SOE downsizing in 1998 during which a staggering number of more than 35 million workers were laid off
  • Our DID identification strategy relies on the heterogeneity across provinces in the realized intensity of SOE layoffs
  • Following theoretical guidance, we document the effect of the massive SOE downsizing on the number and performance of new entrepreneurs

Preview of Results

  • Consistent with theoretical prediction, in pre SOE downsizing, the ability ranking is SOE > Entrepreneurship > POE
  • the 1998 SOE downsizing boosted new firm creation as well as the entry of new entrepreneurs
  • the layoff-induced entrepreneurs outperformed other comparable entrepreneurs, heterogeneity across cohorts is consistent with theoretical prediction
  • 4 alternative stories are tested: business environment, labor cost, labor quality, and social capital accumulation

Take aways

  • The large SOE sector, through its provision of additional benefits, leads to a reluctance towards entrepreneurial endeavors among high skilled individuals
  • Once these previously reluctant, high-skilled individuals are compelled to exit the SOEs, they transform into successful entrepreneurs
  • The allure of state-sector employment for high-skilled labor not only discourages the growth of the private sector through labor allocation but, more importantly, impedes the establishment of new productive private firms on a broader scale

Topic 4-2 Growing Like China

Paper: Market Economy, Chinese Style

The puzzle

The combination of high growth and high return to capital, and a growing foreign surplus are puzzling

  • Open economy: a large net capital inflow rather than an outflow, owing to the high domestic return to capital
  • Closed economy: high investment rate would lead to a fall in the return to capital

Key motivating facts

  • Since 1992, China has built a huge surplus of foreign reserves:
    • From 20 billion in 1992 to ca. 2.5 trillion USD
    • A significant share of the global imbalance
  • Fast growth in GDP pc
    • No tendency for the rate of return to capital to fall in spite of a very high investment rate
  • Others
    • Wage growth below productivity growth
    • Growing inequality
    • Rising saving rates

The rate of return to capital

![截屏2023-10-25 17.27.32](typora_photo/截屏2023-10-25 17.27.32.png)

No evidence of decreasing rates of return to capital is at odds with the prediction that rates of return should fall due to the high investment rate.

Key structure

  • Differences in rates of return across firms (e.g., private sector vs. SOE)
  • Strong asymmetric financial market imperfections (limited access of private firms to bank financing)

Main prediction

  • Gradual labor and capital reallocation within manufacturing (decline of SOE and expansion of private firms)
  • Non-decreasing returns to investment
  • Accumulation of a foreign surplus
  • Cross-regional correlation between net surplus and DPE employment share
  • Low wage growth and rising inequality

Evidence for the first assumption

  • Broad agreement that SOEs have lower returns than private firms
    • Lower value added per unit of capital in SOE

Evidence for the second assumption

  • Ample evidence that private firms face financial constraints
    • Capital-output ratios are 2.6 times larger in SOE than in private firms
    • SOE finance larger share of investment by bank loans

An overall picture of SSZ model

  • Two sources of economic growth
    • the non-state sector is more productive
    • it attracts more labor from the state sector, as it gradually accumulates capital over time
  • The role of the financial system
    • mainly supports the state sector and has a minor role for the non-state sector
  • The model captures important features of Chinese Economy
    • Superior efficiency of non-state sector over state sector
    • Asymmetric support of banking system
    • joint presence of rapid economic growth and a rising foreign reserve

What’s missing?

  • An investment driven economy after 2008
    • state sector as the government’s key control of the economy
  • State sector plays a major role in executing capital-intensive infrastructure projects
    • necessary environment for non-state sector to grow, positive externality
    • supports a system of private firms, which are centered around the state firms
  • Financial system directly supports the infrastructure projects
    • indirectly supporting the whole economy
    • financial repression through low interest rate

Debate on Expansion/ Contraction of state sector

  • “Red Capitalism” by Carl Walter and Fraser Howie
    • through various government policies and programs, such as grasping the large and releasing the small & the post-crisis stimulus, the state sector has expanded, rather than contracted during the recent years
    • In particular, it managed to form monopoly in certain key industries
  • “Markets over Mao” by Nicholas Lardy
    • despite a temporary expansion of the state sector in 2009, its role in the economy has substantially decreased
  • Data measurement in a tricky issue in studying Chinese economy
    • how to define the state sector?
      • a common way is based on firm legal registration as state-owned
    • Hsieh and Song (2015) point out two problems
      • many state-owned firms are legally registered as foreign firms, such as Shanghai-GM, Shanghai-VW
      • many state-owned firms, particularly after 1998, are registered as limited-liability or publicly traded companies
  • A precise picture of bank lending to state and non-state firms is lacking
    • by stock, loans to state firms are substantially larger
    • By flow, new loans to non-state firms are quickly catching up
      • some indication that loans to non-state firms tend to be more expensive and have shorter maturity

An extended view of state sector

  • The state sector may be more than just a reserve of labor for the fast-growing non-state sector
  • Capital-intensive infrastructure building
  • Supports a system of private firms in both down-stream and up-stream of the chain
  • The government’s position on the state sector
    • key to control the commanding height of the economy
    • continuing effort to reform and strengthen the state sector
    • will the financial system continue to serve as a key instrument to support the state sector?

Kuznets Curve

Possible Mechanisms for KC

Early in the development process, investment opportunities for those who have money multiply, while an influx of cheap rural labor to the cities holds down wages.

As economy further develops, human capital accrual takes the place of physical capital accrual as the main source of growth; and inequality slows growth by lowering education levels because poorer, disadvantaged people lack finance for their education in imperfect credit-markets. Financial market development will start to relieve the credit market imperfection, leading to more qual oppotunity in education.

Piketty

He argues that Kuznets mistook the 1930-1950 decrease in inequality for the endpoint of its development. Since 1950, inequality has again reached pre-WW II levels. Similar trends are visible in European countries.

  • Global income inequality, as measured by the ratio T10/B50 between the average income of the top 10% and the average income of the bottom 50%, more than doubled between 1820 and 1910, from less than 20 to about 40, and stabilized around 40 between 1910 and 2020.

Topic 5-2 Tale of Transition

Paper: A tale of transition: an empirical analysis of economic inequality in urban china, 1986-2009 (By: Haiyan Ding and Hui He)

History of Economic Inequality in China

  • The rapid growth of China’s economy in the past 3 decades has resulted in a widening economic inequality gap
    • Variance of log household disposable income in China increased from 0.14 to 0.43 in 1986-2009 - the fastest increase of any country covered in RED (Review of Economic Dynamics)
  • Unlike other countries, they find that consumption inequality tracks closely with income inequality over time (most other countries, like the US, have consumption inequality increasing slower than income inequality)
  • Why do they track closely? Two possible reasons:
    • Existence of “hand to mouth” consumers, i.e., people who consume what they earn, consumption equals income
    • A change in the income shock structure due to economic transition

Permanent income shock: long term change in income due to injury, job change, etc.

Transitory income shock: temporary change in income due to temp, sickness, leave, bonuses, etc.

Why is there a change in shock structure? Because of major Chinese Economic Events:

1978: Open-door policy initiated by Deng, economic reform

1984: After household responsibilities system reform in rural China, economic reform shifts focus to cities

1989: Economic crisis triggered after political turmoil following corruption and inflation

1992: Reform pushes China towards market economy, as privatization begins

1997: Private sector eventually surpasses state sector in GDP share, leading to SOE privatization through “grasp the large, let go of the small” policy

Paper Research Questions

  • How do consumption inequality and income inequality change over time in China?
  • What are the causes for these changes over time?

Their answers:

  • Income inequality and consumption inequality have been rapidly increase in China
  • Particularly surprising: co-movement between income and consumption inequality
  • Explanation: Permanent income shocks are dominating transitory income shocks as a result of economic transition in China, leading to less risk-sharing across individuals

Income Dynamics

  • Over time, there is very limited risk sharing across individuals
  • Thus, consumption inequality closely tracks with income inequality
  • Possible theories:
    • “Hand to mouth” consumers
    • Underlying income shock structure
      • Rising permanent income shocks dominate transitory income shocks over time

Theory 1: “Hand-to-Mouth”

  • Theory implies:
    • Consumption would be roughly equal to income
    • Variance is roughly equal
    • Saving rate should be close to 0
  • Problems:
    • Average household saving rate is close to 20% over time
    • Saving rate has been increasing over time

Except in the lowest income quintile, we see that the household saving rate is significantly positive in all income quintiles

Theory 2: Underlying Income Shock Structure

  • It is harder for households to insure against idiosyncratic permanent income shocks than against transitory income shocks
  • If rising permanent income shocks dominate transitory income shocks over time, the uninsurable idiosyncratic income shocks increase over time
    • impedes efficient risk-sharing among households to smooth consumption
    • tight co-movement between income and consumption inequality
  • It is getting more difficult to share risk across individuals over time, leading to synchronization between consumption and income inequality

Transitory versus Permanent Income Shocks

Similar: Variance of transitory shocks is higher than permanent shocks

Different: Permanent income shock estimated by the level method is far more volatile, dramatic increase in variance of permanent income shock

WHY?

Economic transformation to push the economy toward a more market orientation

SOE closure - disadvantaged groups were hurt more severely by the transition

Income Shock: SOEs vs. POEs

  • Workers of non-SOEs face a significantly higher and more volatile income shocks than SOE worker

  • Substantial increase in permanent income variance that SOE workers faced from 1994 to 2000 could be a consequence of the SOC restructuring reform

Income Shock by education

Less-educated group has higher permanent and transitory income variance than the group with at least an associate degree

Income Shock by age

  • Permanent income shock still shows an upward time trand for both groups
  • Older group tends to have a higher and more volatile permanent income variance

Conclusion and Further Recommendations

  • Drastic increase in economic inequality from 1986 to 2009
  • Consumption inequality closely tracks with income inequality over time
    • Goes against standard consumption smoothing theory
    • Estimate inequalities over the life cycle
  • Possible explanation is fundamental change in idiosyncratic income shock structure
    • More difficult for individuals to insure against income shocks
  • Could be “growing pain” associated with economic transition

Motivation

  1. Challenges

hybrid economy

hybrid structure of China’s real estate sector

The government controls the supply of land, which is the primary input for real estate development, and local governments use revenue from land sales to fund infrastructure and urban development projects. Meanwhile, real estate properties are sold to households and firms in a relatively free market environment. Banks provide debt financing (either directly through bank loans or indirectly through purchasing publicly issued bonds) to all participants in the real estate sector: local governments, which often use future land sale revenues and unsold land as collateral to obtain bank loans; real estate firms, which use land holdings and unsold real estate properties as collateral to finance ongoing projects; households, which take mortgage loans to finance their housing purchases; and firms, which use real estate properties as collateral to finance their real estate purchases or other investments.

The hybrid structure may lead to overinvestment and overleverage in China’s real estate sector due to several forces. 1. 2. short-term incentives

Ultimately, the path to derisk China’s real estate sector is to find new growth engines for the Chinese economy. One proposed solution is to transform China’s economy to be more driven by internal consumption rather than exports. However, this transition necessitates the bolstering of consumer confidence and enabling consumers to power the economy, which may not align seamlessly with the state-driven economic model.

In addition to finding new growth engines, policymakers must also find a new financing model for local governments, replacing the reliance on land sale revenues.
